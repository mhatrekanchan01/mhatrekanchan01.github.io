What I Learned Building a Slack + Zapier HR Check-in Bot

A small automation does not need to replace HR judgment. Sometimes the best use of automation is simply making sure the right conversation happens at the right time.

In HR, a lot of important work fails not because people do not care, but because the process depends too much on memory. Someone has to remember who needs a check-in, when the last conversation happened, what was discussed, and what needs follow-up.

That works for a small group for a short period of time. It does not scale well when teams are distributed, calendars are full, and HR is handling multiple regions.

I built a simple Slack + Zapier workflow to solve this kind of problem. The goal was not to make the employee experience robotic. The goal was to make the operational layer more reliable.

The workflow helped trigger check-in nudges, capture responses, and create better visibility into follow-ups. Instead of manually remembering every touchpoint, the system created a basic rhythm. HR could then spend more time on the actual judgment: reading the response, spotting patterns, and deciding what needed attention.

The biggest lesson was that useful HR automation does not need to be complex. It just needs to remove the repeated manual step that creates inconsistency.

This kind of workflow is also a good example of where HR teams can use automation without losing the human part of the work. The bot does not replace the conversation. It protects the process around the conversation.

What I would improve next

  • Add cleaner status tracking
  • Create a better manager summary view
  • Add tags for recurring themes
  • Connect check-in responses to a dashboard
  • Build escalation logic for items that need follow-up

The takeaway: HR automation works best when it supports consistency, not when it tries to replace context.

Dashboards Beat Memory: Turning Employee Check-ins into Regional Signals

Employee check-ins create a lot of useful information, but without structure, the insights disappear into notes.

One of the problems with employee check-ins is that they create a lot of valuable but unstructured information. Someone mentions workload concerns. Someone else talks about manager communication. Another person flags confusion around career growth. Individually, each conversation may seem small. Collectively, they can reveal the health of a team.

The challenge is that HR notes are often not built for pattern recognition. They are written for the moment, not for future reporting.

I started building a structured check-in tracker to solve this. The idea was simple: every check-in should leave behind usable signals. Not just a paragraph of notes, but fields that can later be filtered, summarized, and reviewed.

The tracker included sentiment, key themes, follow-up items, watch areas, region/team view, last connect date, action owner, and summary notes.

This changed the value of the check-in process. Instead of relying on memory, I could look at the data and ask better questions: which themes are repeating, which employees have not had a recent touchpoint, which items are open, which teams may need attention, and what should be summarized upward.

This is where HR operations and structured people support overlap in a useful way. The human conversation still matters, but the system makes the information easier to use.

The biggest lesson was that a dashboard does not need to be fancy to be useful. A well-structured spreadsheet can be enough if it helps HR move from scattered notes to visible signals.

The takeaway: If the work matters, it needs a system. Otherwise, it becomes memory-based HR.

A Small Meal Budget Analysis That Made the Decision Obvious

Not every People Ops analysis needs to be complex. Sometimes a small spreadsheet is enough to make a better decision.

A meal stipend issue can sound minor until it becomes a repeated employee experience problem. Employees were frequently paying out of pocket for office meal orders, but before recommending a change, I wanted to understand the actual data.

The current office meal budget was $20. I reviewed 82 DoorDash orders over a three-month period to see how often employees were exceeding that amount.

The numbers made the issue clear: the average order value was $25.02, 73 out of 82 orders were above the $20 budget, 89% of orders required some employee top-up, the average employee top-up was $5.25, and total employee out-of-pocket amount was $430.46.

I then modeled what would happen if the budget increased to $25. Out-of-pocket orders would drop from 89% to 44%, total employee out-of-pocket amount would reduce from $430.46 to $155.06, and the estimated incremental annual company cost was approximately $1,102.

This made the decision easier to discuss. Instead of saying “employees are unhappy with the lunch budget,” the recommendation could be framed with numbers: here is the current employee impact, here is the proposed change, and here is the estimated cost.

The analysis was not complicated. That was the point. People Ops teams do not always need advanced analytics to make better decisions. Often, they need clean data, a clear comparison, and a practical recommendation.

The takeaway: Small operational data points can be powerful when they are translated into decision-ready language.

The HR Work No One Sees: Making Vendor Data Trustworthy

A lot of HR operations work is invisible until something goes wrong. Vendor data reconciliation is one of those areas.

Some of the hardest HR operations work is not flashy. It is the work of making sure the data is right before anyone sends an answer.

This shows up often in multi-country HR operations. An employee sees one leave balance in the HRIS. A vendor or EOR has another calculation. A local rule affects carryover or expiry. A manager wants a quick answer. The employee wants clarity. HR has to make sense of all of it.

The real work is not just checking a number. It is understanding the logic behind the number.

For leave balances, that can mean looking at original entitlement, accruals, carryover, expiry rules, utilization, adjustments, HRIS history, and vendor source-of-truth calculations.

Once the numbers are reconciled, the next challenge is communication. Employees do not need a confusing internal audit trail. They need a clear explanation of how the balance was calculated and which system is considered the source of truth.

This kind of work has taught me that HR operations needs both detail orientation and translation skills. It is not enough to be correct internally. The answer also has to be understandable.

It also showed me why process documentation matters. If each case is solved from scratch, HR becomes slow and inconsistent. But if the logic is documented, future questions become easier to handle.

The takeaway: Good HR operations is often invisible. But when the systems, data, and explanations are clean, everything downstream works better.
